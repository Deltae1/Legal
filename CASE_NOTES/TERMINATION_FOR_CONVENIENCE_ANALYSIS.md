## What "Termination for Convenience" Means in Federal Contracting

"Termination for convenience" (T4C) is a specific federal acquisition regulation (FAR) mechanism:
- **FAR Clause 52.249-1 through 52.249-4** govern termination for convenience
- It allows the government to end a contract without cause — purely at the government's discretion
- It is NOT a performance termination — it carries zero negative implication about the contractor's work
- The contractor is entitled to settlement costs including fees earned, work performed, and certain costs

**Key distinction from other terminations:**
- Termination for default = contractor did something wrong
- Termination for convenience = government chose to end it — contractor did nothing wrong

USAGM chose this language deliberately. It is a legal admission that:
1. Elvin did nothing wrong
2. The termination was the government's unilateral choice
3. No performance, conduct, or capability justification exists

## Why This Is Significant for Elvin's Case

### 1. Eliminates Any Performance Defense — Permanently
USAGM cannot now argue Elvin was terminated for performance, conduct, or capability reasons.
They chose "termination for convenience" — that language is theirs, it's in writing, and it forecloses any after-the-fact performance narrative.

### 2. Entitles Elvin to Settlement Costs Under FAR
Under federal acquisition regulations, a contractor terminated for convenience is entitled to:
- All fees/costs incurred up to termination date
- Settlement of the contract
- Reasonable costs of winding down

**Has Elvin received a proper T4C settlement? If not — that is a separate claim.**

### 3. The DOGE Justification Is Now Doubly Void
- "Termination for convenience due to DOGE cuts" = the stated reason
- A federal court has ruled the DOGE cuts were illegal
- Termination for convenience requires a legitimate government purpose
- An illegal budget action is not a legitimate government purpose
- Therefore: the T4C itself may be legally void

### 4. T4C + Illegal Underlying Order = Void Termination
The legal chain:
1. DOGE order issued → directs USAGM cuts
2. USAGM issues T4C to Elvin citing DOGE cuts
3. Federal court rules DOGE order was illegal
4. T4C based on illegal order = T4C without valid basis = potentially void

This is not a stretch — federal courts have invalidated T4C actions where the underlying government decision was unlawful.

### 5. T4C vs. Administrative Leave — The Critical Distinction
The court ruling referenced "employees on administrative leave" returning to work.
Elvin received a T4C — a harder separation.

**This matters because:**
- If the court order covers admin leave employees only, Elvin may not be automatically covered
- However his T4C, based on an illegal DOGE order, is independently voidable
- An attorney can argue his T4C should be treated the same as admin leave given the illegal underlying basis
- Alternatively: filing a separate action to void the T4C is viable

### 6. Selective Non-Recall After T4C
Even accepting the T4C as valid for argument's sake:
- Programming resumed
- Others were recalled
- Elvin — the highest-rated employee, with approved FMLA and accommodation — was not

A T4C does not prevent the government from rehiring. The decision not to rehire Elvin while rehiring others is a separate, affirmative act that must be justified independently.

## FAR Settlement Rights — Action Item

Under FAR 49.1, a contractor terminated for convenience has the right to submit a **settlement proposal** to the contracting officer. This includes:
- Costs incurred
- Profit on work performed
- Settlement expenses

**Has Elvin submitted a T4C settlement proposal?**
- If not — this right may still be available depending on the contract's limitations period
- A federal contracts attorney (in addition to employment attorney) should review this
- The settlement process is separate from the discrimination/retaliation claims
