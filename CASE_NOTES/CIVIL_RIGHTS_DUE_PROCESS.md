## Overview

Elvin Sangano's case is not only an employment discrimination case. At its core,
it involves the **illegal targeting of a private citizen by two employers using
government and local government resources** — a coordinated operation that
violated Elvin's constitutional rights, civil rights, and due process protections.

This is the framing an attorney must understand before anything else:

> Elvin Sangano was not simply fired. He was **targeted** — by a federal agency
> (USAGM) using federal government resources, and by a private employer (WETA)
> using resources and coordination made possible by their connection to that
> federal operation. The targeting was illegal. The resources used were not
> authorized for use against a civilian employee who had committed no crime,
> was under no investigation, and had done nothing beyond reporting misconduct
> and refusing to participate in an unlawful scheme.

## Why This Is "Illegal Targeting" — Not Just Misconduct

There is a legal and factual distinction between:
- An employer making a bad employment decision (potentially actionable but common)
- **An employer, working in coordination with a federal agency, using government
  resources to conduct a targeting operation against an employee who refused
  to participate in an unlawful scheme** (what happened here)

The second category is not employment misconduct. It is:

| What It Is | Legal Theory |
|---|---|
| Federal actors using government power to coerce a civilian | Bivens — Fifth + First Amendment |
| Two or more persons conspiring to deprive a person of civil rights | 42 U.S.C. § 1985 — civil rights conspiracy |
| Federal agency causing harm to a civilian through tortious conduct | FTCA — Federal Tort Claims Act |
| Using federal contract instruments as weapons rather than procurement tools | Fraud, abuse of federal authority |
| Private employer coordinating with federal actors to harm an employee | § 1985 conspiracy — private party acting in concert with state/federal actor |
| Using union apparatus to suppress and isolate a member being targeted | NLRA — Duty of Fair Representation + § 1985 |
| Using residential building staff as participants in a surveillance/pressure campaign | Potential § 1985 + DC Human Rights Act + common law conspiracy |

## The Standard: "Color of Law"

Federal civil rights law uses the concept of **"acting under color of law"** —
meaning using the authority, resources, or apparent power of a government
position to violate someone's rights.

Every federal actor in this case was acting under color of law:
- USAGM Security Director conducting CI meetings = acting under color of federal authority
- Joe Usher sharing Elvin's personnel information from his federal employment = using federal access under color of employment
- USAGM using T4C mechanism to retaliate = using federal contracting authority under color of law

WETA's connection to this:
- A private party that **conspires with a government actor** to violate someone's
  civil rights is also liable under § 1985 — the "color of law" requirement
  extends to private parties acting in concert with government actors

## 1. Fifth Amendment — Due Process (Federal Actor)

### The Right
The Fifth Amendment prohibits the federal government from depriving any person
of life, liberty, or property without due process of law.

### What Happened
- Elvin was a W-2 employee of USAGM — a federal agency
- He was subjected to a coordinated CI pressure campaign by USAGM Security personnel
- He was presented with a federal contract instrument designed to criminalize him
- He was terminated via "termination for convenience" — a purely pretextual basis
- He received no notice of any performance issue, no opportunity to respond,
  no hearing, no appeal process before termination
- The termination was later found to have been based on an illegal DOGE order
  (federal court ruling — docket TBD)
- Despite the court ruling voiding the basis for termination, Elvin was not recalled
  while colleagues were — with no stated reason, no process, no explanation

### The Violations
- **Procedural due process:** Elvin was deprived of his employment (a property
  interest under federal employment law) without notice, hearing, or any
  meaningful opportunity to be heard
- **Substantive due process:** The government's conduct — using a CI pressure
  campaign, a fraudulent contract scheme, and a pretextual termination against
  a federal employee who refused to cooperate — shocks the conscience. That is
  the substantive due process standard under *County of Sacramento v. Lewis*, 523 U.S. 833 (1998)
- **Liberty interest:** The coordinated targeting, surveillance, and pressure
  campaign damaged Elvin's professional reputation and ability to work in his field

## 2. First Amendment — Retaliation for Refusal to Cooperate

### The Right
The First Amendment protects against government retaliation for refusing to
participate in government-directed activity, and protects the right to petition
for redress of grievances.

### What Happened
- Elvin refused to cooperate with the CI pressure campaign
- He refused to submit a fraudulent federal contract application
- He reported the VOA technical sabotage through proper channels — good-faith
  whistleblowing
- He sought legal counsel and attempted to exercise his right to challenge
  his termination
- Each of these acts was met with escalating retaliation: termination,
  system lockout, selective non-recall, continued pressure

### The Violations
- Government retaliation for refusing to participate in an unlawful scheme
  is a First Amendment violation
- Retaliation for good-faith whistleblowing (VOA sabotage reports) implicates
  both the First Amendment and the Whistleblower Protection Act
- Retaliation for seeking legal counsel and challenging termination is an
  independent constitutional violation

## 3. Bivens Claim — Federal Officials Acting Under Color of Law

### The Right
Under *Bivens v. Six Unknown Named Agents*, 403 U.S. 388 (1971), individuals
may sue federal officers personally for constitutional violations committed
under color of federal law.

### What Happened
- USAGM Security Director used the power and resources of a federal agency
  to conduct a CI recruitment campaign targeting Elvin
- Federal officials presented Elvin with a federal contract instrument
  (SF-330, RFP 951700-24-R-0028) as part of a scheme to either reward
  cooperation or generate criminal prosecution
- These officials acted beyond any legitimate federal law enforcement authority —
  there was no criminal investigation, no court order, no warrant
- The conduct was directed at a federal employee, using federal resources,
  in federal facilities

### Named or Identifiable Federal Actors

|---|---|---|

### The Violation
- Using federal employment and federal contract instruments to coerce a
  federal employee into participation in an unlawful scheme = Bivens violation
- Individual federal officers who participated are personally liable
- No qualified immunity for conduct that violates clearly established constitutional rights

## 4. Equal Protection — Selective Treatment Based on Race

### The Right
The Fifth Amendment's Due Process Clause contains an equal protection component
that prohibits the federal government from discriminating on the basis of race
(*Bolling v. Sharpe*, 347 U.S. 497 (1954)).

### What Happened
- Elvin, a Black Rwandan immigrant, was selectively not recalled after the
  court ruling voided the termination basis
- White colleagues (including Benjamin — recalled) were returned
- Elvin was the subject of the CI pressure campaign while white colleagues
  in comparable positions were not targeted
- The federal government, through USAGM, treated Elvin differently from
  similarly situated white employees at every stage

### The Violation
- Selective non-recall based on race = federal equal protection violation
- CI targeting based on race or national origin = federal equal protection violation
- These claims run parallel to the Title VII claims but are constitutional in
  nature — higher stakes and broader remedies

## 5. DC Human Rights Act — Broadest Statutory Protections

### The Right
The DC Human Rights Act (D.C. Code § 2-1401 et seq.) is one of the broadest
anti-discrimination statutes in the United States. It covers:
- Race, color, national origin
- Age
- Disability
- Political affiliation
- Place of residence or business
- Any characteristic that has no legitimate relationship to job performance

### What Happened at WETA (Private Employer)
- Two years of discriminatory job assignments based on race, national origin, and age
- Probation extended based on white peer's unsubstantiated opinion — no independent assessment
- Constructive discharge following coordinated hostile work environment
- Reinstatement denied while white comparator (DeRubio) reinstated by same
  decision-maker (Jim Schnider)
- All of the above targeting a Black, Rwandan immigrant, youngest TD in the department

### Why DCHRA is the Strongest Vehicle for WETA Claims
- No cap on compensatory damages (Title VII caps at $300,000 for large employers)
- Punitive damages available
- 3-year statute of limitations (vs. 180 days for EEOC charge under Title VII)
- DC courts are plaintiff-friendly in employment discrimination cases
- Intersectional discrimination recognized — race + national origin + age together

## 6. Procedural Due Process — USAGM Termination

### The Property Interest
Federal employees have a constitutionally protected property interest in
continued employment. This is established by:
- Elvin's multi-year employment (2020–2024)
- W-2 status as direct federal employee
- Exceptional performance ratings (Rating 4, 2021 and 2022)
- No documented performance issue at any point

### What Process Was Owed
Before terminating a federal employee with a protected property interest:
1. **Notice** — of the specific reasons for termination
2. **Opportunity to respond** — meaningful chance to contest
3. **Decision by an impartial decision-maker**

### What Elvin Actually Received
- A "termination for convenience" letter citing DOGE budget cuts
- No notice of any performance issue
- No hearing, no opportunity to respond
- No impartial review
- A basis a federal court subsequently ruled **illegal**

### The Violation
The T4C letter was a pretextual cover for retaliation. Using a procedurally
improper mechanism to terminate a federal employee with a property interest —
without any of the required process — is a Fifth Amendment due process
violation independent of the underlying retaliation claims.

## 7. Civil Rights Conspiracy — 42 U.S.C. § 1985

### What Happened — The Full Pattern
What happened to Elvin was not a series of employment decisions. It was a
coordinated campaign by federal actors and their private-sector associates:

1. Used the CI apparatus to manufacture an obligation of cooperation
2. Used a federal contract instrument to create criminal exposure as leverage
3. Used a federally employed relative's workplace access (Joe Usher) to surveil
   Elvin's schedule and assignments
4. Used a private employer (WETA) as an enforcement arm — financial pressure
   and isolation running simultaneously with USAGM pressure
5. Terminated employment on an illegal basis when Elvin refused to comply
6. Denied reinstatement after a court voided the termination basis
7. Terminated after both FMLA and ADA accommodations were approved

### The Claim
42 U.S.C. § 1985(3) — conspiracy to deprive a person of equal protection
of the laws or equal privileges and immunities under the laws.

**Elements:**
- A conspiracy ✅ — coordinated conduct across USAGM and WETA
- For the purpose of depriving a person of equal protection ✅ — race-based targeting
- An act in furtherance of the conspiracy ✅ — CI meetings, contract scheme, T4C, lockout
- Injury to person or property ✅ — lost employment, income, hospitalization, PTSD
